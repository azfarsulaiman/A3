Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

Simplicity, I have found, is a powerful tool to seamlessly communicate design. It served as an important guiding principle for my design choices in A3 to utilize readers’ existing mental models. I wanted my chart to effectively answer the question posed, while still leaving room for diverse interpretation. By focusing on how the trend evolves in my visualization across different buckets, I sought to encourage deeper research in the why aspect, referenced as “Explain the Data” in this week’s readings. The data, straightforward as it was, I wanted to be expressed immensely and information to be easily perceived. Hence, I utilized the classic population pyramid but transformed it to enable richer comparisons and interpretations.

My intention was to use the pyramid to target accessibility and interpretation by a wider audience and I enhanced my visualization with certain design encodings. First, I binned the data into 5 age clusters (Child, Young Adult, Middle Age, Senior and Super Senior) to enable shared insights for classes sharing characteristics. Also, this approach would keep the chart simple, effective and prominently highlight trends. Next priority was to effectively utilize the space. Reflecting on lectures, I used conventions to facilitate eye-reading and perspectives. The x-axis span represented U.S. population with females on the left of the y-axis and males on the right to contrast the two. The age clusters were denoted on the y-axis and on left-most side for progression and bottom-up reading of the graph. I kept the gridlines, slightly grayed, with the bars starting at point 0 to serve as benchmark population levels and enable accurate comparisons. Legend, again orthodoxly, was kept on the top-right. Third, I employed bars because they represent aggregation better than any other mark; an important communication choice for population metrics. The series overlap was 50% while the gap width was 150% to allow lumping as per respective age clusters but overlap to simplify comparisons along inter-year and inter-sex dimensions. This can be seen how insignificant it is for 1900s male and female population in Super Senior cluster indicating that clearly something happened that raised population for same demographic in 2000. But I still wanted a certain distinction to be represented hence placed the bars slightly higher/lower for sex. Building on this distinction, I used the conventional colors for sex (albeit stereotypical) with the darker hues for 2000 and lighter for 1900, building on existing mental models. The main focus was to express easier identification of population trends for the reader. Hence, these color choices were taken along with representing all other information (labels, titles, axes) in black.

Reflection

My major question of analysis for the dataset was What was the age-wise population mix of maels and females in the years 1900 vs 2000?

I was also conscious of enabling further research with my visualization and wanted to depict important quantitative information. Hence, I added labels to the bars to indicate magnitude and nominal numerical. The priority was highlighting ordinal comparisons to be read off the bars, but I included quantitative data labels (lower font to play-down its significance on the reading). Also, since the information has a fixed zero, I also had the choice to include quantitative (ratio) representation but decided against it. I was wary of cognitive load on the reader and didn’t want to overwhelm with too information, just down-played its significance to still leave the door open for anyone interested in diving deep in my design. The titles, labels and legend were hence suitably named to supplement this philosophy. I crafted these choices to allow easier trend representations to emerge, anticipating a pyramid shape for the visual trends represented by the scale and extent of bars across age clusters, encouraging further reflection on why, for the respective genders a century apart.
